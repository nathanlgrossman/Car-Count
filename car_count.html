Discussion

The above plot of the car count time series seems to show seasonality, as there appear to be cyclical variations in the series, as well as longer term trends, as the central tendency of the series appears to increase between 2010 and 2012, and then to decrease between 2013 and 2016. The initial upward trend may refect the recovery of the economy after the Great Recession, while the subsequent downward trend may reflect the decline in retail during the latter half of the decade (but these observations are highly speculative).

Since the magnitude of the cyclical variation does not appear to vary dramatically over time with the central tendency, an additive model of the series in terms of trend and seasonal components appears appropriate (or at least more appropriate than a multiplicative model as a first pass). Since the parking lot is situated at a retail location, and since consumer behavior varies during the week and over the year, both weekly and annual seasonality components appear plausible.

Time Series Decomposition

Decompose car count time series assuming an additive model with a trend component and a seasonal component, of the form
y(t) = b(t) + s(t) + e(t)
where b(t) is the trend, s(t) is the seasonality and e(t) is the error

First, assume the dominant seasonality (cyclicality) is weekly, so there are 7 (daily) observations per season (cycle)

Create time series object under weekly seasonality assumption

countTS7 = ts(dataDF$car.count, freq=7)

Decompose time series into trend and weekly seasonal components

countComps7 = decompose(countTS7, type="additive")
plot(countComps7)

Second, assume the dominant seasonality (cyclicality) is annual, so there are 365.25 (daily) observations per season (cycle)

Create time series object under annual seasonality assumption

countTS365 = ts(dataDF$car.count, freq=365.25)

Decompose time series into trend and annual seasonal components

countComps365 = decompose(countTS365, type="additive")
plot(countComps365)

Decompose car count time series assuming an additive model with a trend component and two seasonal components, using a TBATS state space model of the form
y(t) = l(t-1) + phi * b(t-1) + s1(t) + s2(t) + e(t)
where l(t) is the level, phi is a damping parameter, b(t) is the trend, s1(t) and s2(t) are the seasonalities, and e(t) is the error
For more details on TBATS models, see “Forecasting time series with complex seasonal patterns using exponential smoothing” by Hyndman, De Livera and Snyder

Create multi-seasonal time series object under weekly and annual seasonality assumption

countMSTS = msts(countXTS, seasonal.periods=c(7,365.25))

Create TBATS state space model with trend, weekly and annual components

countTBATS = tbats(countMSTS)

Extract trend, weekly and annual seasonal components

countCompsTBATS = tbats.components(countTBATS)
plot(countCompsTBATS)

Discussion

Reviewing the above plot of the series decomposition with weekly seasonality (the first of the three plots in this section), we observe that the trend varies at a higher frequency than we would expect. This may be due to the annual cyclical variations being aliased as variations in the trend, since the model does account for the annual seasonal component directly.

Reviewing the above plot of the series decomposition with annual seasonality (the second of the three plots in this section), we observe that the trend is more consistent with our a priori expectation than was the case for the decomposition with weekly seasonality. This may indicate that, if we are limited to a single-seasonality decomposition, then annual seasonality is a more natural choice than weekly seasonality.

Reviewing the above plot of the series decomposition with weekly and annual seasonality (the third of the three plots in this section), we observe that the trend is basically consistent with our a priori expectation, but contains more high frequency variation than was the case for the decomposition with annual seasonality only. This may be an artifact of the fact different algorithms were used for the multiple-seasonality and single-seasonality decompositions, and degree of smoothing or high frequency filtering applied may differ. If we had more time, we would investigate this possibility.

The behavior of the annual seasonal component of the multiple-seasonal decomposition (labelled “season2” in the plot) may be explainable, at least in part, by our intuition about how consumer behavior varies over the course of the year. That is, there is a peak of activity at the end and very early begining of the year, possibly corresponding to the holiday buying season, followed by a sharp decline.

Discussion

First we predicted and plotted the car count time series using the TBATS model with both weekly and annual seasonal components. Reviewing the above plot of the prediction with weekly and annual seasonality, we notice that the predicted series seems to reflect the seasonal components of the previously observed series, but seems to assume no change in central tendency due to long term trend. If we had more time, we would investigate this further.

Next we created predictive models using TBATS with either a weekly component or an annual component, for sake of comparison. We observe that the training set RMSE for the weekly-and-annual seasonal model is 47.26, while the RMSE is 47.62 for the weekly seasonal model, and is 47.67 for the annual seasonal model. While this indicates that the multiple-seasonal model performs better than either of the single-seasonal models, we would investigate whether the magnitude of the performance difference should actually be larger if we had more time.

Last we created a predictive model using linear regression, with car count as the target, and year, month, day of week and cloud indicator as categorical predictors. This may be an unconventional approach for time series data, but we took this approach because: (1) it provided a relatively simple way to incorporate information about the other variables observed into our prediction of car count; and (2) it allowed us to analyze the data from a different perspective while using a well-understood predictive algorithm. The R-squared value for this model was 0.62, indicating that there is potential this approach to predicting the car count. The residual standard error is 31.01. Future work would include investigating whether the residual standard error reported by the linear model in R is directly comparable to the RMSE values reported by the TBATS models.

Moreover, future work would focus on the more fundamental challenge of creating a model which combines trend and seasonality information from the car count time series, with information from the other variables observed (i.e. weather and cloud indicator), to obtain more accurate predictions.
